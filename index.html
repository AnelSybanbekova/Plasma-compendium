Atomic Physics Basics

Structure of the Atom

All matter consists of molecules, and molecules consist of atoms. An atom is the smallest unit of a chemical element that retains its chemical properties.

According to Rutherford's model, an atom consists of a positively charged nucleus surrounded by electrons. The electrons move around the nucleus and occupy discrete energy levels.

Figure 1. Structure of the atom according to Rutherford's model.

The Atomic Nucleus

The nucleus contains positively charged protons and electrically neutral neutrons. Together, protons and neutrons are called nucleons.

Protons and neutrons are not elementary particles. They are composed of quarks. A proton consists of two up quarks and one down quark (uud), while a neutron consists of one up quark and two down quarks (udd).

Limitations of Rutherford's Model

Although Rutherford's planetary model successfully described the structure of the atom, it could not explain several experimental observations.

  1. According to classical electrodynamics, an electron orbiting the nucleus should continuously emit electromagnetic radiation and lose energy. Therefore, it should eventually fall into the nucleus. However, atoms are stable.
  2. Rutherford's model predicted a continuous atomic spectrum, whereas experiments showed that atoms emit and absorb radiation only at specific wavelengths, producing line spectra.

Bohr's Model

These problems were resolved by Bohr's model of the atom.

Bohr proposed that electrons can occupy only specific allowed energy levels. While an electron remains in one of these stationary states, it does not emit radiation.

Radiation is emitted or absorbed only when an electron transitions between two energy levels. The energy of the emitted or absorbed photon is equal to the difference between these levels.

What is Plasma?

Plasma is often called the fourth state of matter. It consists of free electrons and ions and exhibits collective electromagnetic behavior.

Plasma Parameters

Important plasma parameters include density, temperature, Debye length, plasma frequency, and collision rates.

Plasma Confinement

Plasma can be confined using magnetic or inertial methods. Examples include tokamaks, stellarators, and laser-driven fusion systems.

Plasma Diagnostics

Diagnostics are techniques used to measure plasma properties, including Langmuir probes, spectroscopy, interferometry, and Thomson scattering.

Current Research Challenges

Modern plasma research focuses on fusion energy, turbulence, plasma-wall interactions, advanced diagnostics, and high-performance simulations.
