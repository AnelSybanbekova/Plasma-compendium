Electron Plasma Oscillations

Plasma consists of ions and negatively charged electrons. If electrons are slightly displaced, local electric fields appear, creating regions with an excess or deficiency of electrons.

The electric field tends to restore the electrons back to their original position. Due to the inertia of electrons, an oscillatory process begins. This process is similar to a spring oscillation, where the electric field plays the role of the restoring force.

Ion Acoustic Waves

Ion acoustic waves are collective oscillations where ions move while electrons are redistributed and create an electric field. This process is similar to sound waves in a gas.

vs = √((kTe)/mi)

where:

  • Te – electron temperature;
  • mi – ion mass;
  • k – Boltzmann constant (1.38 × 10-23 J/K).

Particle Motion in Magnetic Fields

The presence of a magnetic field changes particle motion due to the Lorentz force:

F⃗ = q(v⃗ × B⃗)

This force causes charged particles to rotate around magnetic field lines. The radius of this circular motion is called the Larmor radius:

rL = (m v⊥)/(qB)

where:

  • v⊥ – velocity perpendicular to the magnetic field;
  • B – magnetic field strength.

If particles also have velocity along the magnetic field, they move along a helical trajectory with the cyclotron frequency:

ωc = (qB)/m

Without a magnetic field, electron and ion oscillations dominate plasma behavior. In the presence of a magnetic field, additional phenomena appear, such as Alfvén waves and cyclotron waves.

Magnetic Plasma Confinement

Plasma confinement is one of the main challenges in fusion research. Because plasma reaches temperatures of several million kelvin, no material can directly withstand contact with it.

Therefore, magnetic confinement was proposed, where plasma is held away from reactor walls using magnetic fields.

Charged particles move along magnetic field lines and rotate in Larmor orbits. The basic condition for confinement is:

rL ≪ L

where L is the characteristic size of the plasma.

This means that particles are strongly bound to magnetic field lines and cannot easily escape across the magnetic field.

Tokamak

A tokamak is a device for magnetic plasma confinement. The plasma has the shape of a torus.

A toroidal magnetic field is created by external coils. A current flowing through the plasma generates an additional poloidal magnetic field. Together, these fields create spiral magnetic lines along which particles move.

Stellarator

A stellarator is another magnetic confinement device. Unlike a tokamak, the magnetic field is created completely by external coils, meaning that a plasma current is not required.

Plasma Instabilities

Plasma is an unstable system because it contains free charged particles, conducts current, interacts with magnetic fields, and exists at high temperatures and pressures.

Small disturbances can lead to turbulence, disruption of the plasma column, and energy losses.

Main types of instabilities:

  • Kink instability – bending of the plasma column.
  • Sausage instability – compression and expansion of plasma.
  • Drift instability – caused by temperature and density gradients.
  • Magnetohydrodynamic instability – interaction between plasma and magnetic field.

Plasma Stability

Thermal stabilization is related to the distribution of temperature and pressure inside plasma:

p = nkT

where:

  • n – particle density;
  • k – Boltzmann constant;
  • T – plasma temperature.

Methods of thermal stabilization include uniform heating and density control.

Plasma Beta

Magnetic stabilization is described using the plasma beta parameter:

β = (2μ0p)/(B²)

where:

  • p – plasma pressure;
  • B – magnetic field induction;
  • μ0 – magnetic permeability of free space.

In tokamaks, β ≈ 0.1–0.2, which corresponds to good magnetic confinement.

Laser-Plasma Interaction

Lasers can be used to create plasma by transferring energy to matter. Electrons are heated, atoms become ionized, and plasma is formed.

The interaction depends strongly on plasma density. The critical density ncr determines how the laser beam interacts with plasma.

For densities below ncr, the laser passes through the plasma with little interaction.

For densities above ncr, electrons at the surface shield the laser field, preventing deeper penetration and reducing the beam intensity.

Therefore, materials with near-critical density, such as low-density foams, are used. The laser penetrates into the material, ionizes it, and creates plasma efficiently.
