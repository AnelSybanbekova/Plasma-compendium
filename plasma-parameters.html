Plasma Parameters

The main properties of plasma are quasi-neutrality, electrical conductivity, collective particle behavior, Debye shielding, and plasma oscillations.

Quasi-Neutrality

Plasma is generally electrically neutral because the number of electrons is approximately equal to the number of positive ions:

ne ≈ ni

Local charge imbalances can occur and generate electric fields and currents, but on a macroscopic scale plasma remains electrically neutral.

Electrical Conductivity

Plasma consists of charged particles and therefore has high electrical conductivity. It responds strongly to electric and magnetic fields, making it possible to control plasma using electromagnetic fields.

Collective Behavior

In plasma, particles interact primarily through electric and magnetic fields, unlike neutral gases where interactions occur mainly through collisions. As a result, plasma exhibits collective phenomena such as plasma waves, turbulence, and instabilities.

Debye Shielding

Debye shielding is a phenomenon in which charged particles rearrange around an introduced charge and weaken its electric field.

The characteristic distance over which this shielding occurs is called the Debye length, λD.

At distances smaller than λD, the surrounding particles cannot fully compensate for the charge. At distances larger than λD, the electric field of the original charge decreases significantly.

Plasma Oscillations

Plasma oscillations are collective oscillations of electrons relative to ions. The plasma frequency is given by:

ωp = √[(nee²)/(ε0me)]

where:

  • ne – electron density;
  • e – elementary charge;
  • me – electron mass;
  • ε0 – vacuum permittivity.

Cold and Hot Plasma

Hot Plasma

Hot plasma is a plasma in which the temperatures of electrons, ions, and neutral particles are approximately equal:

Te ≈ Ti ≈ Tn

Such plasma is close to thermodynamic equilibrium and reaches temperatures of millions of kelvin. Fusion devices operate at temperatures of approximately 107–108 K.

Hot plasma is found in the Sun, other stars, fusion reactors (tokamaks), lightning, and nuclear explosions.

Cold Plasma

Cold plasma is a plasma in which the electron temperature is much higher than the temperatures of ions and neutral particles:

Te ≫ Ti ≈ Tn

Electron temperatures typically reach 104–105 K, while ions and neutral particles remain close to room temperature (~300 K).

This occurs because electrons are much lighter than ions and gain energy rapidly from electric fields, while transferring relatively little energy to heavier particles.

Cold plasma is widely used in plasma medicine, plasma lamps, surface treatment, and semiconductor manufacturing.
